# Article Source Packet

Use this before commissioning or drafting a public short article. The packet
tests whether the material is strong enough to become a publishable piece.

## Metadata

- Working title:
- Source window:
- Source logs or artifacts:
- Reader-world research packet:
- Requested audience:
- Reporter:
- Status: sufficient / insufficient

## Core Incident

Describe what actually happened in sequence.

Include:

- what started the work
- what the author thought the problem was
- what changed
- what the real problem turned out to be
- what happened next

## Human Protagonist

- Who wanted something?
- What did they want?
- What pressure were they under?
- What would failure cost?

## Exact Moment

Name the specific point where the story became difficult, awkward, revealing,
or surprising.

Include:

- file, note, log entry, message, meeting, question, or artifact
- what the author expected to see
- what was actually there
- what became newly clear

## Visible Artifacts

List concrete objects a reader can picture.

Examples:

- a thin log entry
- a before/after note
- a meeting question
- a customer email
- a deleted paragraph
- a metric table
- a rule that could not work

## Artifact Availability

For every named public artifact, record:

- artifact:
- direct link:
- if unavailable, pulled, archived, paywalled, or known only through secondary
  reporting, what source establishes that:
- how the article will say this to the reader:

Block drafting if the article would reference a public artifact without either
a direct link or a clear availability note.

## Reader-World Bridge

Identify a real reader-world example or source-backed parallel.

Do not use a generic hypothetical as the main bridge. If no real bridge exists
yet, mark the packet insufficient.

Summarize the linked reader-world research packet:

- 6 to 10 scene seeds gathered:
- sources checked:
- strongest recognition patterns:
- examples rejected because they have been used before:
- selected reader-world examples for this article:

The bridge should help the reader recognize the problem before the article
states the thesis. One anecdote from the author may be enough for an opening,
but it is not enough to set the wider table unless the reader-world research
packet shows why the audience will recognize the pattern.

## Stakes

Name the palpable downside if the problem is ignored.

Include at least one:

- bad decision
- wasted time
- damaged trust
- false confidence
- loss of control
- embarrassment
- work that cannot compound
- money or reputation at risk

## Counterpoint Or Objection

Name the smartest objection.

Examples:

- "Isn't this just bureaucracy?"
- "Does this slow down useful work?"
- "Do we really need to save this much?"
- "Is the article overselling a small internal process?"

## Surprise Or Turn

What changed the author's understanding?

Do not accept a thesis that was obvious before the source material was read.

## Only-I-Could-Write-This Details

List details that could not be generated by a generic AI article.

Include at least three before the packet can be marked sufficient.

## Missing Material

List what still needs to be gathered before drafting.

## Scene Cards

Link required scene cards.

- `scene-cards/001-...md`
- `scene-cards/002-...md`

## Example Ledger Check

Link `docs/education/articles/example-ledger.md` and confirm:

- no major anecdote from a previous article is reused as the opening, main
  evidence, or primary bridge
- repeated examples, if any, are minor references only
- selected examples are recorded or ready to be recorded after drafting

## Quality Gate

Answer:

- Is there enough material for a short magazine-standard article?
- Would the piece need an invented opening or generic bridge to work?
- Does the material support a fresh thesis?
- Is the strongest story from this window better than publishing nothing?
- Are named public artifacts linked or explicitly marked as pulled,
  unavailable, archived, paywalled, or known only through secondary reporting?
- Has the reader-world research packet produced enough examples to set the
  table before the thesis?
- Can a cold reader understand the world, object under pressure, AI temptation,
  downside, and personal relevance by roughly line 30?

Decision:

- sufficient
- insufficient: scan a wider source window
- insufficient: gather more source material
- insufficient: no publishable article this cycle
