# Reader-World Research Packet

Use this before drafting any public short article for a non-technical or
lightly technical audience. The packet gathers examples from the reader's
world so the article can set the table before it teaches the harness story.

This packet is not a quote bank. It is a recognition bank: sourced situations,
objects, pressures, and language that help the writer understand what the
reader may have already seen, felt, or quietly worried about.

## Reader Recognition Patterns

List patterns that appear across the sources.

Examples:

- polished summary forwarded without evidence
- AI-written proposal based on shaky assumptions
- meeting notes that erase disagreement
- roadmap, forecast, or board update made calmer than the facts
- team decision that cannot be reconstructed later

For each pattern, name the concrete object, social pressure, and possible
downside.

## Setting-The-Table Options

Name 2 to 3 ways the article could build the reader's world before moving into
the author's source story.

For each option include:

- opening or early supporting situation
- why the reader would recognize it
- how it leads into the source story
- what it risks making too broad or too familiar

## Example Ledger Check

Review `docs/education/articles/example-ledger.md`.

Record:

- major anecdotes already used
- examples too close to reuse
- examples selected for this article
- examples retired after this article

Do not reuse major anecdotes from prior articles. Minor references may only be
reused when they are not doing the main explanatory work.

## Line 30 Cold-Reader Gate

Before drafting, answer what a cold reader should understand by roughly line
30:

- what world they are in
- what kind of person this happens to
- what object, document, meeting, message, or decision is under pressure
- why AI makes the situation tempting
- what can go wrong
- why they should personally care

If these cannot be answered from the research and source packet, mark the
packet insufficient.
