# Short Magazine Article Quality Bar

Use this quality bar for public short articles. The target is not a longer
blog post. The target is a compact, magazine-standard essay or explainer: one
clear story, enough source material to feel lived, and prose that carries the
reader from scene to meaning without generic smoothing.

This rubric is operational, not decorative. If an article cannot meet the
material gates, do not draft it. It is better to publish nothing from a weak
cycle than to publish a fluent piece with no real story.

## Source-Backed Criteria

### Source Material Density

Based on The Open Notebook's emphasis on pre-reporting, concrete details,
sources, key questions, likely answers, and elements the article is likely to
include.

- 5: The packet contains a specific incident, visible artifact, concrete
  before/after, reader-world research packet, counterpoint, and details that
  could only come from the author's actual experience or named source
  evidence.
- 4: The packet contains a real incident, visible artifact, concrete stakes,
  enough source detail to draft without inventing the main bridge, and enough
  reader-world research to set the table.
- 3: The packet has a real source arc but relies on summary more than scene; it
  needs one or two more concrete details before drafting.
- 2: The packet is mostly internal process summary, with no visible artifact or
  reader-world bridge.
- 1: The packet contains a thesis but no reportable material.

Block below 4.

### Artifact Availability

Based on The Open Notebook's reporting emphasis on source material and UNC's
reader-expectation test: readers should know what evidence is available to
inspect and what is being mediated through reporting.

- 5: Every named public artifact is linked directly or explicitly marked as
  pulled, unavailable, archived, paywalled, or known only through secondary
  reporting; the article never implies direct access it does not have.
- 4: Every central artifact has a clear access route or availability note;
  minor supporting artifacts may need a cleaner note.
- 3: Sources are linked, but the status of at least one important artifact is
  ambiguous to the reader.
- 2: The piece relies on a public artifact the reader cannot inspect and does
  not explain why.
- 1: The piece gestures at artifacts without source routes.

Block below 4.

### Opening Scene

Based on UNC's guidance that introductions should bridge readers from their
world into the piece, and The Open Notebook's ladder-of-abstraction guidance to
begin with concrete, imageable material.

- 5: A concrete, non-hypothetical moment with a human actor, visible object or
  exchange, pressure, and an implied question that pulls the reader forward.
- 4: A real moment with a human actor and clear pressure, though the visual
  detail could be sharper.
- 3: A clear situation, but mostly conceptual; the reader understands the idea
  without feeling present.
- 2: A hypothetical or generalized scene carries the opening.
- 1: Abstract thesis, trend claim, slogan, or mechanism-first opening.

Block below 4.

### Felt Stakes

Based on the requirement that a reader understand why the piece matters, what
is at stake, and why the story belongs now.

- 5: The opening makes a palpable downside visible: lost money, damaged trust,
  wasted time, false confidence, embarrassment, loss of control, or a bad
  decision someone must defend.
- 4: The downside is concrete and early, though it could use sharper human
  consequence.
- 3: The piece names risk but does not make the reader feel who pays.
- 2: The stakes are observations or abstract importance.
- 1: No visible downside.

Block below 4.

### Reader Orientation

Based on UNC's reader-bridge and reader-expectation tests.

- 5: By the end of the opening, a cold reader can say who is under pressure,
  what happened, why it matters, what kind of evidence the article will use,
  and how the situation resembles ordinary work they recognize.
- 4: The reader can follow the situation and stakes with only minor missing
  context.
- 3: The reader can follow the general idea but needs insider knowledge to
  understand why the example matters.
- 2: The piece drops the reader into internal terms or unbuilt settings.
- 1: The reader cannot retell the opening without borrowing the article's
  abstract vocabulary.

Block below 4.

### Reader-World Setting

Based on UNC's reader bridge and The Open Notebook's emphasis on reported
specificity and concrete examples.

- 5: Before or near the nut graf, the article gives 2 to 3 concrete,
  source-backed reader-world situations so the audience recognizes the problem
  before the thesis is stated.
- 4: The article gives at least two concrete reader-world examples, though one
  could use sharper sourcing or object detail.
- 3: The article gestures at recognizable situations but relies on summary
  more than scene.
- 2: The article uses a generic hypothetical or one thin bridge.
- 1: The article assumes the reader already understands the setting.

Block below 4 for public articles aimed at non-technical or lightly technical
readers unless the editor records a clear exception.

### Line 30 Cold-Reader Gate

By roughly line 30, a cold reader should know:

- what world they are in
- what kind of person this happens to
- what object, document, message, meeting, or decision is under pressure
- why AI makes the situation tempting
- what can go wrong
- why they should personally care

- 5: All six answers are concrete and visible.
- 4: All six answers are present, with one needing sharper detail.
- 3: The reader understands the general issue but cannot name the object or
  downside clearly.
- 2: The article has a strong opening but rushes into abstraction.
- 1: The reader cannot orient without insider context.

Block below 4.

### Thesis Freshness And Angle

Based on The Open Notebook's pitching guidance: the article needs a clear
angle, key questions, likely answers, and a way it goes beyond existing or
obvious treatment.

- 5: The thesis preserves the strongest source arc and gives the reader a
  sharper, less generic way to understand the topic.
- 4: The thesis is specific, sourced, and audience-relevant.
- 3: The thesis is true but could fit many AI articles.
- 2: The thesis is a familiar content-calendar point.
- 1: The thesis is a slogan.

Block below 4.

### Structure And Tension Chain

Based on narrative structure, nut-graf practice, and the need to move between
concrete detail and meaning.

- 5: The article moves through a tension chain: scene, pressure, question,
  deeper problem, complication, earned lesson, and landing.
- 4: The structure has a clear spine and avoids becoming a list of related
  points.
- 3: The article is coherent but mostly explanatory; the middle sags or
  repeats the thesis.
- 2: The piece is a sequence of polished observations.
- 1: The structure is only intro, thesis, explanation, conclusion.

Block below 4.

### Voice Match

Based on the local voice sample bank as primary evidence, not adjectives in the
voice profile.

- 5: The article sounds recognizably like the author: practical authority, dry
  precision, earned bluntness, generosity under pressure, and concrete stakes.
- 4: The voice mostly fits, with a few over-smoothed lines.
- 3: The voice is clear but could belong to any thoughtful AI commentator.
- 2: The voice is polished, generic, or too lecturerly.
- 1: The voice sounds machine-authored.

Block below 4.

### Sentence Craft And Specificity

Based on Purdue OWL's emphasis on effective words, specific language, and
pruning dead weight.

- 5: Sentences use specific nouns and verbs, varied rhythm, and almost no
  filler; abstractions are tied to concrete details.
- 4: Prose is clear and specific, with only occasional soft phrasing.
- 3: Prose is readable but uses too many abstract nouns or repeated cadences.
- 2: Prose is smooth but low-density.
- 1: Prose relies on generic AI cadence, padding, or significance inflation.

Block below 3.

### Ending

Based on UNC's guidance that conclusions bridge readers back to daily life with
lasting meaning.

- 5: Ending returns to a scene, image, changed understanding, or practical
  consequence with earned resonance.
- 4: Ending lands the thesis in a concrete reader-facing consequence.
- 3: Ending restates the thesis clearly but abstractly.
- 2: Ending trails into summary.
- 1: Ending is a slogan.

Block below 3.

## Required Decision

Every public short article review must include:

- scores for each category
- block/approve decision
- reason for every score below 4
- missing material required to raise the score

Do not average the scores to hide a weak core. A piece with weak source
material, weak opening scene, weak stakes, or weak voice match is blocked even
if the prose is clean.
