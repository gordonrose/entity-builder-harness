# Writing Style Principles

Use these as durable quality principles, not as source imitation.

- Prefer plain, specific language.
- Open with a moment, not a generic thesis.
- Use rhythm: short sentences help hard ideas land.
- Keep technical credibility by naming concrete details.
- Remove sentences that could have been written without reading the logs.
- Avoid generic AI phrasing and corporate polish.
- Avoid significance inflation: do not replace specific facts with vague claims
  about importance, legacy, transformation, broader trends, or key roles.
- Avoid superficial analysis that appends generic meaning with words like
  "highlighting", "underscoring", "reflecting", "showcasing", or "serving as".
- Treat positive zeitgeist language as a surface tone, not a substitute for
  evidence. Specificity has to earn the lift.
- Avoid punchy AI syntax: tidy two-sentence hooks, abstract reversals, and
  slogan-shaped contrasts that sound optimized rather than spoken.
- Openings need lived context and implicit authorial stake. Tell the situation
  so the reader understands why it matters before naming the lesson. Do not
  announce "I care" as the point.
- Build the setting before using internal vocabulary. Translate the human
  situation first, then introduce technical terms only when they help.
- Avoid platonic abstraction: do not let "the system", "the process", or "the
  decision" float away from a concrete person, purpose, and consequence.
- Do not confuse "hook" with snap. A strong opening creates a need to keep
  reading by placing a person inside a specific pressure, not by producing a
  neat sentence that could introduce almost any AI essay.
- Put stakes early. If the reader cannot feel the cost, risk, loss, or threat
  to control within the first seven lines, the opening is still throat-clearing.
- Avoid unexplained evaluative shortcuts. Words like "useful", "obvious",
  "important", and "interesting" often mark places where the writer has named a
  value the reader has not been made to feel.
- Avoid actual calendar dates in public article openings unless the exact date
  carries reader-facing stakes. Otherwise the piece reads like a log entry and
  ages faster than it needs to.
- Make the nut graf pay rent. Once the scene has created tension, the nut graf
  should tell the reader what this is about, why it matters now, and what is at
  stake.
- When a term matters, teach it. Do not hide useful vocabulary, but do not name
  it before the reader has felt the plain-language need for it.
