# Voice Sample Bank

This file is the primary voice reference for public articles. The voice profile
describes the target. This bank provides evidence.

## Source Samples Used

- Attached article by Gordon Rose on building trust in difficult stakeholder
  meetings.

## Sounds Like The Author

### Practical Authority

> In product, we are usually the ones who are wheeled out to explain why a
> roadmap or deliverable doesn't/can't meet customer expectations.

Why it works:

- Starts from a recognizable job pressure.
- Uses ordinary, slightly wry phrasing.
- Names an uncomfortable role without self-pity.

Use for:

- openings that begin with a real working predicament
- AI articles where the author is inside the mess, not above it

### Earned Simplicity

> But as in all things, the crux of it was simple:

Why it works:

- Confident but not inflated.
- Sets up a useful list after lived context.
- The simplicity is earned by the preceding story.

Use for:

- transitions from anecdote to framework
- moments where the article has built enough pressure to name the lesson

### Concrete Trust Mechanics

> I made the stakeholder feel heard.

> I proved to them I can do something about at least one of their most pressing
> problems.

> I proved to them I cared enough to keep working on their most painful
> problems until they got sorted.

Why it works:

- Abstract trust becomes a sequence of observable behaviors.
- The verbs are plain and accountable.
- The list feels like experience compressed, not content strategy.

Use for:

- turning AI abstractions into operational tests
- explaining what "evidence", "control", or "trust" looks like in practice

### Stakes With Teeth

> A stakeholder who doesn't trust us will interrupt that work consistently with
> 'accountability overhead'. Constant checkins, escalations, reports, root cause
> analyses, weekly calls, debates, etc.

Why it works:

- Coins a useful phrase after concrete pain.
- Shows cost through familiar behaviors.
- Has dry precision without trying to be funny.

Use for:

- naming hidden AI costs after examples
- avoiding vague claims such as "this reduces productivity"

### Earned Bluntness

> That's a mistake.

Why it works:

- The blunt line lands because the preceding paragraph builds the collision
  between empathy and responsibility.
- It is not a slogan; it is a judgment.

Use for:

- short corrective pivots after evidence
- rejecting attractive but harmful working habits

### High-Stakes Specificity

> One participant shared a story of how they had to fly in to tell a customer
> the product line they had been sold, and had spent close to 7 figures
> integrating with, was due to be sunset in favour of another product line that
> didn't match all of that customer's requirements.

> I once had to get a customer worth 20% of our revenue to prioritize a list of
> 30 features that had been sold as 'already in use by other customers' because
> -in fact- we hadn't even started building them yet.

Why it works:

- The numbers carry the pressure.
- The details make the story hard to fake.
- The author trusts the reader with the ugly bit.

Use for:

- public AI articles that need specific stakes early
- replacing generic "teams may struggle" language

### Generous But Not Soft

> If a customer or stakeholder is shouting at you, getting them stop shouting is
> still a hell of a win.

Why it works:

- Respects the difficulty of the situation.
- Recalibrates success without pretending the problem is solved.
- Sounds spoken and human.

Use for:

- realistic success criteria
- teaching readers without making them feel small

### Mechanism With Relief

> Almost like magic, getting just one thing resolved would more often than not
> take enough pressure off the rest of the list and calm things down just enough
> so the situation starts to feel a little more under control.

Why it works:

- The "magic" is immediately grounded in a mechanism.
- The sentence lets relief enter without becoming fluffy.

Use for:

- describing how small process changes create felt control
- AI pieces that need optimism earned through mechanism

## Preferred Moves

- Open from a recognizable working pressure.
- Put the author in the room when the author was actually in the room.
- Move from story to framework only after the pressure is visible.
- Use short lists when they compress hard-won operating knowledge.
- Let bluntness arrive after evidence.
- Use numbers when they carry real stakes.
- Coin a phrase only after the concrete behavior has been shown.
- Keep empathy practical: people feel heard, options stay open, pressure drops,
  trust buys freedom.

## Public Technical Explainer Examples

### Speed With Accountability

> I really like how quickly AI works. But we shouldn't think that speed doesn't
> come with a lot of downsides.

Why it works:

- Starts from an honest desire rather than a warning.
- Keeps the surface plain and accessible.
- Gives the reader permission to like the speed before introducing the cost.

Use for:

- openings where the reader's attraction to AI is real
- articles that balance capability and control

### Inspectability As The Cost Of Speed

> Work can now be created faster than it can be inspected, understood, tested,
> explained, and owned.

Why it works:

- Names the full accountability chain.
- Makes the bottleneck visible without sounding anti-AI.
- Uses a clean list that escalates from inspection to ownership.

Use for:

- nut grafs about AI output pressure
- explaining why harness structure is not bureaucracy

### Faster Mess

> If AI gives me ten times the output but only one tenth of the inspectability,
> I have not become ten times faster. I have become the owner of a faster mess.

Why it works:

- Converts an abstract productivity tradeoff into a memorable consequence.
- Has dry bite, but the joke serves the teaching point.
- Centers human accountability.

Use for:

- turning productivity claims into accountability tests
- endings or pivots where the article needs a hard landing

### Human Accountability

> The assistant is not accountable. I am.

Why it works:

- Blunt, clear, and load-bearing.
- Names the human ownership problem without melodrama.
- Gives the article a moral center.

Use for:

- transitions from AI capability to human responsibility
- rejecting drafts that make the assistant seem like the accountable actor

## Lines That Do Not Sound Like The Author

> The AI summary sounded good. Then someone asked what it was based on.

Tags: too punchy, too synthetic, fake tension, generic AI cadence.

> There is a stage in every new technology cycle where the vocabulary spreads
> faster than the understanding.

Tags: abstract throat-clearing, content-calendar opening, no lived pressure.

> I was about to give my AI assistant a more responsible job.

Tags: mechanism-first, vague value label, no human pressure.

## Banned Moves

- slogan-shaped contrast before story
- TED-talk uplift
- productivity-influencer rhythm
- abstract "future of X" openings
- fake humility
- fake epiphany
- generic "teams are realizing" authority
- scenes that begin with "Imagine a team" as primary evidence

## Voice Audit Questions

- Which line could only Gordon have written?
- Where is the practical authority?
- Where does the prose show pressure in concrete terms?
- Where is the earned blunt sentence?
- Which coined phrase names a real mechanism?
- Which paragraph sounds like generic thoughtful AI commentary?
- Which sentence is too smooth to be trusted?
