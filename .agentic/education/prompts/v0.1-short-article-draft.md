# v0.1 Short Public Article Draft Prompt

Draft the selected short public article.

Inputs:

- Selected idea
- Relevant extracted material
- Approved article source packet
- Approved reader-world research packet
- Approved scene cards
- Approved article editor brief
- Approved article opening lab
- Article Writer Agent instructions
- Voice profile
- Voice sample bank
- Humor profile
- Storytelling profile
- Prior feedback

Write a compact public article at short magazine-style standard. Default target
length is 800 to 1,200 words unless the user requests otherwise.

Requirements:

- approved article source packet exists before drafting
- approved reader-world research packet exists before drafting for public
  articles that need audience recognition
- approved scene cards exist before drafting
- approved article editor brief exists before drafting
- approved article opening lab exists before drafting
- follow the Article Writer Agent contract
- draft from the selected source scene in the source packet and article editor
  brief
- use the reader-world research packet to set the table with fresh,
  recognizable examples before or near the nut graf
- draft from the opening approved in the article opening lab
- preserve the selected thesis from the brief unless the draft reveals a
  stronger one, in which case stop and revise the brief first
- first person
- clear enough for students
- credible enough for practitioners
- specific to the actual work
- lightly funny, not gag-heavy
- reflective but not pompous
- practical but not bland
- no corporate tone
- no fake epiphany
- no generic opening like "In software development..."
- no punchy AI-syntax hooks or slogan-shaped contrasts
- no abstract opening about technology cycles, eras, trends, or the future
- no internal repo vocabulary before the reader understands why the story
  matters
- no invented hypothetical as opening, main evidence, or primary reader bridge
- no reused major anecdote or example from a previous article as opening, main
  evidence, or primary reader bridge
- no named public artifact without a direct link or an explicit explanation
  that it is pulled, unavailable, archived, paywalled, or known only through
  secondary reporting
- do not write as if the writer inspected an unavailable artifact directly
- no draft notes inside the public article body

Opening requirements:

- Start with a lived situation, not a thesis.
- Write like a person explaining something to another person.
- Use a concrete source scene from the source packet, not a generic scenario.
- Make the reader feel the stakes within the first seven lines.
- Pass the cold reader retell test: after seven lines, a reader should know who
  is under pressure, what went missing or wrong, and what could happen because
  of it.
- Make the reason for caring obvious through the situation; do not state "I
  care" as the point.
- Give the reader a reason to care before naming harnesses, commits, ADRs, or
  logs.
- Before the thesis is stated, give the reader enough setting and examples to
  recognize the problem in ordinary work.
- By roughly line 30, a cold reader should know the world, the object under
  pressure, why AI makes the situation tempting, what can go wrong, and why
  they should personally care.
- If the opening could fit almost any AI article, rewrite it.
- If the opening sounds punchy but not lived, rewrite it.
- If the opening has a scene but no implicit stake, rewrite it.
- If the opening uses file, script, rule, commit, ADR, harness, or log language
  before the human problem is clear, rewrite it.
- If the opening compresses the story into a neat contrast rather than letting
  the reader enter the moment, rewrite it.
- If the opening uses "useful", "obvious", "important", or "interesting"
  before the reader feels why, rewrite it.
- If the opening uses a calendar date and the date itself is not part of the
  stakes, remove it.
- If the opening uses fake vividness, such as a metaphor that decorates an
  abstraction without showing a concrete consequence, rewrite it.
- If a paragraph relies on an internal setting the reader has not been helped
  to imagine, rewrite it.
- Replace repo jargon with plain human equivalents until the reader has enough
  context to benefit from the specific term.
- If the opening names a report, filing, post, study, transcript, or other
  public artifact that readers cannot inspect directly, say that plainly before
  relying on it.

Use this structure:

1. A specific moment
2. The immediate friction
3. Reader-world examples that set the table
4. The problem I thought I was solving
5. The problem I was actually solving
6. The lesson
7. A clean landing

After the draft, include:

- article source packet summary
- reader-world research packet summary
- scene card summary
- article editor brief summary
- article opening lab summary
- source scene used
- selected thesis followed
- reader-world examples used and avoided
- terms translated or delayed
- artifact availability notes
- what works
- what may sound unlike me
- 3 alternate titles
- 3 possible stronger endings
- specificity audit
- voice audit
- opening audit

Write these notes to a separate writer-notes artifact, not into the article
body.
