## Article Premise

The piece argues that the next standard for AI work is not just faster polish.
If an AI-shaped answer is going to travel into a report, customer promise,
meeting recap, filing, ticket, policy, or decision, the work needs enough
evidence underneath it to stand up later.

## Central Story

The public reader-world story comes first: a serious AI report whose citations
failed, a chatbot answer that became a customer dispute, meeting recaps that
need correction, and legal filings with fake cases. These examples establish
the pattern: AI can give weak work the posture of strong work.

Then the article moves to the author's own smaller version. The author wanted
the assistant to decide whether chat decisions needed durable notes, then
discovered the session record was too thin to support that judgment. The work
changed from decision-note checking to evidence capture: questions, issues,
decisions, rationale, log grouping, duration, and token summaries.

The article lands on the standard: the answer has to stand up.

## Real Stakes

False confidence that travels. A weak answer becomes a promise, report,
meeting record, citation, policy, ticket, or decision. The cost appears when
someone else relies on it and asks what it was standing on.

## Why Now

AI work is moving from private prompting into ordinary institutional work
objects: reports, recaps, customer answers, filings, incident reports, and
workflow decisions. The surface story is speed; the deeper standard is
defensibility.
