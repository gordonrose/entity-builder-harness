## Human Predicament

A person or organization puts work into the world with the surface of
authority before the evidence underneath can support it. The bad outcome is
not merely being wrong. The bad outcome is that the work travels because it
looks ready.

## Reader-World Setup

The early article uses:

- KPMG report: serious report, weak citations, institutional authority. The
  original report no longer appears publicly available from KPMG, so the
  article must say this and link the public reporting trail.
- Air Canada chatbot: customer acts on AI answer, company must defend it.
- Meeting recaps: tidy summary becomes the supposed record of what happened.
- Legal filings: formal document with fake cases.

These examples let the reader recognize the pattern before the harness story.

## Opening Candidates

### Candidate 1: Human Consequence

- Draft opening:

  KPMG published a report called *Redefining excellence in the age of agentic
  AI*. It was the sort of thing that arrives wearing institutional authority:
  global consulting firm, confident title, case studies about UBS, the NHS,
  Swiss Federal Railways, and Transport for London, plus a list of citations
  to make the whole thing look anchored.

  Then the anchors started coming loose.

  The report itself no longer appears to be publicly available from KPMG; the
  public trail now runs through reporting about it.

- First concrete noun/action: KPMG, report, case studies, citations
- Who is under pressure: institution and anyone relying on the report
- Artifact availability note needed: yes; say the report no longer appears
  publicly available from KPMG and link public reporting
- Felt stake by line seven: official-looking evidence may not hold up
- Cold reader retell: a KPMG AI report looked authoritative until its case
  studies and citations were challenged
- Abstract words used before they are earned: evidence, but attached to report
  and citations immediately
- Verdict: approved

### Candidate 2: Scene First

- Draft opening:

  The report had citations. That was the point. Citations are the little
  ritual by which a document says, politely, that it is not just making things
  up.

- First concrete noun/action: report, citations
- Who is under pressure: report author and reader
- Artifact availability note needed: yes; otherwise the opening would imply a
  public artifact the reader can inspect directly
- Felt stake by line seven: citations may be only performance
- Cold reader retell: a cited report may not be properly supported
- Abstract words used before they are earned: none serious
- Verdict: strong fallback, but less direct

### Candidate 3: Mechanism First

- Draft opening:

  AI work needs evidence before it can be trusted.

- First concrete noun/action: none
- Who is under pressure: nobody visible
- Artifact availability note needed: none; rejected before source-status
  question matters
- Felt stake by line seven: none
- Cold reader retell: AI needs evidence
- Abstract words used before they are earned: AI work, evidence, trusted
- Why it fails or why it unexpectedly works: it states the thesis before
  recognition
- Verdict: reject

## Comparison

- Candidate 1 makes the cost visible fastest.
- Candidate 1 is most retellable because it has an official report and failed
  citations.
- Candidate 3 relies on abstract system language.
- Candidate 1 best bridges from the reader's world into the source story.
- Candidate 1 is grounded in approved source material rather than an invented
  bridge.
- Candidate 1 gives the reader an honest source route by naming that the
  original report is unavailable from KPMG and linking reporting about it.
- Candidate 1 gives enough runway for Air Canada, meeting recap, and legal
  filing examples before the thesis.

## Editor Decision

Approved to draft from Candidate 1.

Reason: the opening uses a fresh public source, visible object, and immediate
stakes. The draft must not reuse the prior customer anecdote and must build the
reader-world setting before the harness turn.
